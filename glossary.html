Plain Language

The Words, Explained Simply

You don't need to be a scientist to protect your family. Here are the key terms, in plain language.

Technoference

When technology interrupts face-to-face relationships — like a phone buzz during dinner or a notification that pulls your eyes away from a child's question. These small moments add up, weakening connection. Noticing technoference helps us choose to put devices aside and be fully present with the people God gave us.

Dopamine loop

A cycle in the brain where small rewards (likes, notifications, streaks) keep you wanting more. Each buzz gives a tiny feel-good hit, training you to check again and again. Understanding this loop helps us break free from compulsive scrolling and find deeper satisfaction in real connection and God’s presence.

The algorithm

A set of instructions that decides what you see on social media and video apps. It learns what holds your attention and serves you more of it — whether uplifting or harmful. Algorithms don't care about your wellbeing; they only want your time. That's why we must intentionally choose what feeds our mind and heart.

Attention economy

A system where apps compete fiercely for your focus because your attention makes money for them. Every swipe, click, and minute is a product sold to advertisers. Recognizing this helps you stop being the product and start guarding your time for what truly matters: family, rest, and worship.

Absent presence

Being physically with someone but mentally somewhere else — staring at a screen while your family sits nearby. It’s a quiet form of neglect common in the digital age. The remedy is intentional, unhurried connection, especially around the family altar, where we give each other the gift of full attention.

Frontal lobe

The part of the brain just behind your forehead that handles self-control, planning, and decision-making. It doesn't fully mature until your mid-twenties, which is why kids and teens struggle more with screen limits and impulse scrolling. Protecting its development means setting loving boundaries early.

Neuroplasticity

The brain’s amazing ability to rewire itself based on what you practice every day. The more you scroll, the better your brain gets at craving more scrolling. But the more you pray, read Scripture, and focus on meaningful tasks, the stronger those pathways become. This is good news: change is possible with God’s help.

Digital Sabbath

A regular period when you turn off all personal devices to rest and reconnect with God and family. Reflecting the biblical command to set apart unhurried time for renewal, it’s not about legalism but about reclaiming worship, conversation, and creation. Even one evening or a full day a week can restore your soul.

Digital hygiene

Daily habits that keep your technology use clean and healthy, like brushing your teeth. Small consistent actions: deleting distracting apps, keeping phones out of the bedroom, using website blockers, and setting time limits. Good digital hygiene protects your mind, sleep, and relationships from unnecessary wear.

Doomscrolling

The habit of endlessly scrolling through negative news, anxious updates, or upsetting content even when it makes you feel worse. It feeds fear and steals peace. Instead, we can practice turning toward prayer, limiting news intake to short intervals, and filling our eyes with truth and hope.

Deep work

Focused, distraction-free effort on a meaningful task for a set period — the opposite of constant notifications and task-switching. By creating space for deep work, we honor our minds as gifts from God, produce better work faster, and model discipline for our children.

AI companion

A chatbot or digital friend designed to simulate conversation and emotional connection. While interesting, these tools cannot replace real relationships, the comfort of the Holy Spirit, or the wisdom of godly counsel. Use with cautious discernment — they are tools, not confidants for the soul.

Algorithm cleanse

Intentionally resetting what an app shows you by telling it “not interested,” muting certain topics, blocking distracting creators, or taking a break. Over time, the algorithm learns new patterns. This simple practice helps you clean out content that tempts, discourages, or distracts you from guarding your heart.

Digital Babylon

A biblical metaphor for the confusing, noisy, idolatrous pull of modern digital culture. Just as ancient Babylon confused language, the digital world overwhelms us with competing voices, false promises, and endless distraction. Following God means learning to live in it without becoming like it, keeping our worship pure.

The family altar

A daily time when your household gathers to read Scripture, pray, and worship together. It’s not a physical altar but a sacred habit that builds spiritual strength and connection. In a distracted age, the family altar becomes the heartbeat of the home — a non-negotiable space where faith is caught and shared.
